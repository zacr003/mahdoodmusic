## Two Kinds of "Live"

Walk into any electronic music show and you'll see two very different things happening behind the decks — even if they look identical from the crowd.

In one version, a set is loaded, tracks are arranged, and the performer is managing transitions and adding texture. It's a skill. It's not nothing. But the music itself was finished before they walked on stage.

In the other version, the music is *happening* right now. Notes are being chosen. Patterns are being triggered in response to the room. A section gets extended because it's working, or cut short because it isn't.

That second version is what I'm after.

## Why It Matters

I spent years as a classical pianist, which means I spent years learning to reproduce something exactly. The score is the text. Your job is faithful interpretation.

Live electronic performance flipped that entirely. There is no score. The set list is an outline, not a script. What actually happens is determined in the moment by the room, the energy, and whatever decisions I make in real time.

That terrified me at first. Now it's the only thing that feels honest.

## What Makes It Hard

Real-time decision-making under pressure is a skill that classical training doesn't really build. You practice a piece until it's automatic, then you perform the automation. The discipline is in the preparation.

Improvised performance requires a different kind of discipline — knowing your instrument and your material so well that you can *think* while you play, not just execute.

With a rig as large as mine, there's also a practical problem: the more gear you have, the more decisions you have to make per second. Routing, levels, which pattern to trigger next, whether to bring the Prophet in now or wait. It's a lot to manage while also *playing* the keyboard parts live.

I'm still figuring out how to simplify the cognitive load without simplifying the music.

## The Crowd Knows

I don't think audiences can always articulate it, but they can feel the difference between someone playing and someone performing.

When a decision is made in real time — a note choice, a rhythmic shift, a section that runs long because it's *working* — it reads as presence. The performer is in the room with you, making something, not delivering something pre-made.

That's what I want people to walk away with. Not just that they heard something good. That they were *there* when it happened.
