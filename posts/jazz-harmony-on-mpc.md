## Where I'm Coming From

My formal training is in classical piano — I have a Bachelor's in Classical Piano Performance. So I'm not coming to harmony from scratch. I know counterpoint, voice leading, figured bass, the whole thing.

What I'm learning is jazz's *language* — how it uses that same theoretical foundation but bends the rules differently. The vocabulary is familiar. The idiom is new.

## Start With the Shell

A shell voicing uses only the **root, third, and seventh** of a chord. No fifth. No extensions yet.

For a Cmaj7:

- Root: C
- Third: E
- Seventh: B

Play those three notes. That's it. That's a jazz voicing. Notice how the fifth (G) doesn't add much — shell voicings sound cleaner without it.

## Add Tensions One at a Time

Once the shell feels natural, add one tension at a time:

1. **9th** — the note a whole step above the root. On Cmaj7: D. Add it above the shell.
2. **13th** — the note a major sixth above the root. On Cmaj7: A. Sounds lush on major chords.
3. **#11** — the raised fourth. On Cmaj7: F#. This is the Lydian sound — bright and floating.

You don't need all of them at once. One tension changes the character of the chord entirely.

## Voicings in the Hardware Rig

I'm working entirely in hardware — the CK88, Nord Stage 3, and Prophet 6 running into the Tascam Model 12, with the MPC Live 2 handling sequencing. No plugins.

That constraint is useful. When you can't just drag a MIDI clip to a new instrument, you commit to a sound before you play the voicing. The Nord's piano patches reward clean voice leading. The Prophet rewards open, widely-spaced chords — close voicings get muddy fast in the low mids.

> Playing the same voicing on three different instruments teaches you more about the voicing than any amount of theory reading.

When I find something that works, I capture it directly to the MPC as an audio sample. That's the arrangement: recorded moments, not reconstructed MIDI.

## The ii-V-I in Practice

The bread and butter of jazz harmony is the **ii-V-I** progression. In C major:

- **Dm7** — ii chord (minor seventh)
- **G7** — V chord (dominant seventh)
- **Cmaj7** — I chord (major seventh)

Practice moving through these three chords using shell voicings, keeping the voice leading as smooth as possible. The seventh of Dm7 (C) becomes the third of G7. The third of G7 (B) becomes the seventh of Cmaj7. The notes are almost the same — you're just reinterpreting them.

That's voice leading. And that's what separates jazz harmony from chord-slamming.

## Classical Training as a Foundation

The classical background helps more than I expected. Voice leading is voice leading — Bach and Bill Evans are after the same smoothness, just with different rules about what's allowed. Counterpoint training makes you hear inner voices that most producers ignore entirely.

What classical training doesn't prepare you for is the *flexibility* jazz demands. In classical performance, the text is fixed. In jazz, the harmony is a suggestion and the rhythm is a conversation. That's the shift I'm still working on.

I take *one idea* per track — one voicing, one movement — and let it define the character.

A maj9 stabbed on the upbeat. A #11 floating over a drone. A minor-major seventh resolving nowhere. Small moves, informed by a lot of years at the piano.
