## The Decision

The laptop was the last thing to go. I'd already moved most of my production into hardware — the MPC, a small mixer, a few effects boxes. But the laptop stayed because it felt like a safety net.

Then I played a show where the DAW crashed mid-set. Not a small crash. A full, spinning beach ball, "force quit everything" crash. In front of 200 people.

That was the end of the laptop.

## What I Replaced It With

The **MPC Live 2** does everything I needed:

- Sequencing
- Sampling (live and pre-loaded)
- Plugin instruments
- Basic mixing via the onboard outputs

The trick is treating it as a *performance instrument*, not a production tool you happen to play live. That shift in mindset changes everything about how you set up your projects.

## Project Structure

Each live set is its own MPC project. Inside it:

1. **Stems** — pre-arranged sections loaded as audio tracks
2. **Patterns** — 8-bar loops I can trigger on the fly
3. **Live instruments** — piano routed through a MIDI channel for real-time playing

The stems give me a backbone. The patterns give me flexibility. The live instrument gives me the moment.

## The No-Laptop Rule

No laptop means no Ableton, no Splice, no internet-dependent anything. If it doesn't live on an SD card or in the MPC's internal storage, it doesn't exist on stage.

This constraint sounds limiting. It's actually liberating. You stop fiddling with software during soundcheck and start *listening*.

> The best performances I've ever given were on nights when something broke and I had to improvise around it. Constraints are creativity.

## Gear List

- Yamaha CK88
- Nord Stage 3 73
- Sequential Prophet 6
- Tascam Model 12 mixer
- MPC Live 2 (standalone)
- 2 powered speakers

It's a lot of hardware. Setup is not a quick process — but every piece earns its place.

## Would I Go Back?

No. Not even close.

The laptop was a crutch that let me *avoid* preparation. Hardware forces you to make decisions before you walk on stage. And made decisions are better performances.
